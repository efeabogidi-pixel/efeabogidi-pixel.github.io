GIS Case Study

Mapping Extreme Urban Heat Vulnerability — Phoenix, Arizona

A tract-level vulnerability workflow that combines heat exposure, vegetation, demographic context, and access to cooling resources to highlight where risk may be concentrated.

Goal

Create a clear, decision-ready vulnerability output that helps identify which neighborhoods may face higher heat risk and why — using transparent, repeatable GIS steps.

What I built

A tract-level composite index that integrates: land surface temperature (exposure), NDVI (mitigation), demographic vulnerability, and cooling access.

Deliverables

  • Exposure map (LST)
  • Vegetation map (NDVI)
  • Cooling access indicator
  • Final vulnerability index map
  • StoryMap narrative for communication

Why it matters

Heat impacts are uneven. Neighborhood conditions and resource access can amplify exposure, especially for vulnerable populations. GIS helps show patterns clearly so planning and response can be targeted.

Method

High-level workflow (designed to be repeatable and easy to explain).

1) Setup & Study Area

  • Defined Phoenix study area and tract geography
  • Standardized projections and cleaned layers
  • Clipped data to consistent extent

2) Extract Environmental Indicators

  • Prepared LST raster for exposure mapping
  • Prepared NDVI raster for vegetation / mitigation mapping
  • Summarized raster values to census tracts (zonal statistics)

3) Add Social Vulnerability Context

  • Joined demographic variables to tracts
  • Verified joins and performed basic QA checks
  • Created mapped indicators for comparison

4) Cooling Access

  • Prepared cooling center points
  • Computed proximity / access indicator (distance or service logic)
  • Summarized access at the tract level

5) Normalize & Combine

  • Normalized indicators to a common scale
  • Combined variables into a composite vulnerability index
  • Reviewed results for face validity and mapping clarity

6) Communicate Results

  • Designed clean thematic maps with consistent classification
  • Wrote a StoryMap narrative: problem → method → results → implications
  • Documented assumptions, limitations, and next steps

Results & Interpretation

How the outputs can be used and what they show at a high level.

What the maps reveal

The project highlights neighborhoods where higher heat exposure and lower mitigation can overlap with demographic vulnerability and limited cooling access — producing a clearer picture of where intervention may matter most.

How it can be used

  • Prioritize outreach and heat response planning
  • Support cooling center placement and access improvements
  • Guide vegetation and shade investments
  • Communicate risk clearly to non-technical audiences

Limitations

  • Temporal alignment between datasets can affect interpretation
  • Index weighting choices can change rankings
  • Access indicators depend on travel assumptions and context

Next steps

  • Run sensitivity checks on weights and normalization
  • Add canopy / impervious surface / shade variables
  • Incorporate heat-health outcomes if available
  • Ground-truth with local context and stakeholders
